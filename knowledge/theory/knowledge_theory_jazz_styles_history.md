# Jazz Styles and Historical Context

## Bebop (1940s)

### Characteristics
- **Fast tempos:** 180-300+ BPM
- **Complex harmony:** Extended chords, alterations
- **Virtuosic technique:** Rapid eighth-note lines
- **Chromatic approach tones:** Enclosures, chromaticism
- **Bebop scales:** Added chromatic passing tones

### Key Artists
- **Charlie Parker:** Alto sax, creator of bebop language
- **Dizzy Gillespie:** Trumpet, harmonic innovator
- **Bud Powell:** Piano, defined bebop piano style
- **Thelonious Monk:** Piano, unique angular approach
- **Max Roach:** Drums, polyrhythmic innovation

### Bebop Vocabulary
- **Enclosures:** Surround target note from above and below
- **ii-V-I patterns:** Essential progressions in all keys
- **Bebop scale:** Major scale + chromatic passing tone between 5-6
- **Chromaticism:** Approach notes, passing tones
- **Rhythmic displacement:** Syncopation, accents on off-beats

### Practice Focus
Play Parker's "Confirmation," "Anthropology" at tempo. Master ii-V-I patterns in all 12 keys.

## Modal Jazz (1950s-60s)

### Characteristics  
- **Static harmony:** Few chord changes, one mode per section
- **Emphasis on melody/rhythm:** Less focus on changes
- **Scales over chords:** Dorian, Mixolydian, Phrygian modes
- **Pedal points:** Sustained bass notes
- **Open voicings:** Quartal harmony (fourths)

### Key Albums & Artists
- **Miles Davis - Kind of Blue (1959):** Defining modal album
- **John Coltrane - A Love Supreme (1965):** Spiritual modal jazz
- **Herbie Hancock:** Modal piano, electronic exploration
- **McCoy Tyner:** Quartal voicings, Dorian sounds
- **Wayne Shorter:** Modal composition

### Modal Approach
- **"So What" (Dorian):** 16 bars D Dorian, 8 bars Eb Dorian, 8 bars D Dorian
- **Emphasis:** Develop motifs, explore mode, less "running changes"
- **Techniques:** Pentatonics, fourths, open sounds

### Practice Focus
Play "So What," "Maiden Voyage" - stay in mode, develop simple motifs into longer ideas.

## Hard Bop (1950s-60s)

### Characteristics
- **Blues and gospel influence:** Soulful, earthy
- **Medium tempos:** 120-180 BPM (more accessible than bebop)
- **Strong groove:** Emphasis on rhythm section
- **Pentatonic scales:** Blues scales, simpler melodies
- **Extended forms:** Longer modal sections

### Key Artists
- **Art Blakey & Jazz Messengers:** Definitive hard bop group
- **Horace Silver:** Funky, bluesy piano
- **Clifford Brown:** Lyrical trumpet
- **Cannonball Adderley:** Soulful alto sax
- **Lee Morgan:** "The Sidewinder" - hard bop hit

### Hard Bop vs. Bebop
- **Bebop:** Intellectual, fast, complex
- **Hard Bop:** Soulful, grooving, accessible
- Both use similar harmony, different feel

### Practice Focus
Learn "Moanin'" (Art Blakey), "Song for My Father" (Horace Silver). Focus on groove and blues feeling.

## Cool Jazz (1950s)

### Characteristics
- **Lighter tone:** Softer, less aggressive than bebop
- **Slower/medium tempos:** Relaxed feeling
- **Classical influences:** Counterpoint, arrangement
- **Subdued emotion:** Intellectual, cerebral
- **West Coast:** Associated with California

### Key Artists
- **Miles Davis - Birth of the Cool (1949):** Seminal recording
- **Gerry Mulligan:** Baritone sax, pianoless quartet
- **Chet Baker:** Lyrical trumpet, soft vocals
- **Dave Brubeck:** Classical-influenced piano
- **Paul Desmond:** Smooth alto sax

### Cool vs. Hot
- **Hot (Bebop/Hard Bop):** Fast, loud, intense
- **Cool:** Relaxed, soft, restrained

### Practice Focus
"So What," "Take Five" - focus on space, lyricism, restraint. Less is more.

## Post-Bop (1960s)

### Characteristics
- **Harmonic innovation:** Beyond standard changes
- **Metric modulation:** Complex time signatures, rhythm
- **Expanded forms:** Through-composed, less 32-bar AABA
- **Modal and tonal blend:** Both approaches combined
- **Freer improvisation:** Less tied to changes

### Key Artists
- **Wayne Shorter:** Complex compositions
- **Herbie Hancock:** Harmonic adventurer
- **Tony Williams:** Revolutionary drummer
- **Joe Henderson:** Outside playing
- **Miles Davis Second Quintet (1964-68):** Defining group

### Post-Bop Innovations
- **Coltrane Changes:** Rapid key center movement
- **Side-slipping:** Temporarily play in wrong key
- **Superimposition:** Impose different harmony on changes
- **Free time:** Rubato, no strict tempo

### Practice Focus
"Footprints" (Shorter), "Dolphin Dance" (Hancock) - explore harmonic ambiguity, rhythmic freedom.

## Free Jazz (1960s)

### Characteristics
- **No fixed harmony:** Often no chord changes
- **Collective improvisation:** All players equal
- **Extended techniques:** Screams, multiphonics, noise
- **Energy over structure:** Intensity, emotion primary
- **Atonality:** No key center

### Key Artists
- **Ornette Coleman:** Pioneer of free jazz
- **John Coltrane - Ascension (1965):** Free collective improv
- **Cecil Taylor:** Atonal piano approach
- **Albert Ayler:** Spiritual free jazz
- **Sun Ra:** Cosmic philosophy

### Free vs. Structured
Free jazz abandons traditional jazz structure. Less common for beginners; understand changes first.

### Approach
Focus on sound, texture, energy rather than notes. Not recommended until solid changes foundation.

## Fusion / Jazz-Rock (1970s)

### Characteristics
- **Electric instruments:** Electric piano, bass, guitar
- **Rock/funk grooves:** Straight eighths, backbeat
- **High energy:** Loud, amplified
- **Odd meters:** 7/4, 5/4, complex rhythms
- **Synthesizers:** Electronic sounds

### Key Albums & Artists
- **Miles Davis - Bitches Brew (1970):** Fusion pioneer
- **Weather Report:** "Birdland" - fusion hit
- **Herbie Hancock - Head Hunters:** Funk-jazz
- **Return to Forever (Chick Corea):** Latin-fusion
- **Mahavishnu Orchestra:** Heavy rock-jazz

### Fusion Approach
Rock feel, jazz improvisation. More accessible to rock listeners.

## Contemporary/Modern Jazz (1980s-Present)

### Characteristics
- **Eclectic:** Draws from all eras
- **World music influence:** African, Latin, Indian
- **Complex harmony:** Continued post-bop explorations
- **Groove-based:** Funk, hip-hop influences
- **Compositional focus:** Through-composed pieces

### Key Artists
- **Brad Mehldau:** Reharmonization master
- **Kurt Rosenwinkel:** Modern guitar voice
- **Chris Potter:** Contemporary saxophone
- **Robert Glasper:** Hip-hop/jazz blend
- **Esperanza Spalding:** Bass, vocals, genre-bending

### Modern Trends
- Reharmonization of standards
- Odd meters, polyrhythms
- Extended harmony
- Cross-genre fusion

## Stylistic Recommendation by Player

### Your Tempo → Suggested Style
- **60-100 BPM:** Ballads (Bill Evans, Keith Jarrett)
- **100-140 BPM:** Cool Jazz, Medium Swing (Miles Davis, Getz)
- **140-200 BPM:** Bebop, Hard Bop (Parker, Blakey)
- **200+ BPM:** Fast Bebop (Parker, Gillespie)

### Your Note Density → Suggested Style
- **Sparse (<2 notes/sec):** Modal (Coltrane modal period), Cool Jazz
- **Medium (2-4 notes/sec):** Hard Bop, Swing
- **Dense (4+ notes/sec):** Bebop, Post-Bop

## Practice Recommendations by Era

**Bebop Foundation:**
1. Learn Parker solos from Omnibook
2. Master ii-V-I in all keys at tempo
3. Practice bebop scales

**Modal Exploration:**
4. Play "So What" - explore D Dorian for 16 bars
5. Practice staying in one mode, developing motifs

**Contemporary:**
6. Learn modern players (Mehldau, Potter)
7. Explore reharmonization
8. Study odd meters
